# Readability + Attention Span Conversion Brief

## Purpose

This document explains the theory and implementation behind building pages, ads, emails, and product copy that customers can understand quickly, trust quickly, and act on quickly.

The goal is not to make people read more. The goal is to make the important message impossible to miss.

For JobFilter-style products, the customer is busy, sceptical, distracted, and usually not browsing for fun. They need to know:

1. Is this for me?
2. What problem does it solve?
3. Why should I trust it?
4. What do I do next?
5. What do I lose if I ignore it?

---

## The Main Theory

### 1. People scan before they read

Most users do not read websites word-for-word. They scan for cues of relevance. A page should be designed so that a skim-reader still understands the offer.

### 2. The F-pattern

Users scan across the top, then across a lower section, then down the left side.

- Put the strongest claim at the top.
- Start headings with the most important words.
- Keep key phrases toward the left.
- Avoid burying the offer in the middle of paragraphs.
- Use short paragraphs and bold lead-ins.

### 3. Layer-cake scanning

Users jump between headings, only dipping into body when a heading catches interest.

- Every section heading must carry meaning on its own.
- Avoid vague headings like "Our Solution" or "Features".

Bad: `Features`
Better: `See real building work before your competitors call them`

### 4. Spotted-pattern scanning

Users scan for cues: numbers, prices, words they care about, proof, locations, names, guarantees, buttons.

- Use numbers: "12 approved extensions near B12".
- Use location signals.
- Use proof signals: screenshots, examples, sample leads, data source labels.
- Use clear pricing.
- Use strong CTA buttons.

### 5. Cognitive load reduction

- One page = one main action.
- One section = one message.
- One card = one idea.
- One CTA repeated.
- Replace clever language with clear language.

### 6. Plain language wins

Bad: `Optimize your lead acquisition infrastructure with qualified demand intelligence.`
Better: `Stop wasting evenings quoting people who were never serious.`

### 7. Message hierarchy

1. Pain — name the costly problem.
2. Outcome — show the desired result.
3. Mechanism — explain how it works.
4. Proof — show why it's believable.
5. Offer — make the action simple.
6. Risk reversal — reduce fear.
7. Urgency — explain why now matters.

For JobFilter:

1. Pain: "You waste evenings chasing ghost quotes."
2. Outcome: "See serious building work before your competitors."
3. Mechanism: "We scan planning, EPC and council signals."
4. Proof: "12 approved projects found in your postcode."
5. Offer: "Try 2 free scans this week."
6. Risk reversal: "Cancel anytime. No shared lead trap."
7. Urgency: "Founding price ends after first 30 firms."

## Practical Implementation Framework

### The 5-Second Test

A customer should understand the page within five seconds.

Hero formula: `Stop [painful action]. Find [valuable outcome] using [unique mechanism].`

Example: `Stop driving to ghost quotes. Find real building work using council and planning data.`

### The Skim Test

Read only headings, buttons, bold text, and numbers. If the page still makes sense, it passes.

Every heading must: name a pain, show a result, explain mechanism, prove trust, or push action.

### The One-Action Rule

One main action per page. For JobFilter: Run a postcode scan / Unlock a sample lead / Join the founding offer.

Primary CTA: `Scan My Postcode`
Secondary CTA: `See Example Lead`

### The Attention Ladder

- **Layer 1 (Instant):** Headline, subheadline, CTA, proof number, visual card
- **Layer 2 (Skim):** Section headings, bullets, icons, cards, before/after
- **Layer 3 (Deep):** FAQs, detailed explanation, data source notes, case studies, pricing logic

Most users only consume Layer 1 and 2.

---

## Readability Rules

- **Sentences:** Short. Mix rhythm. No long winding paragraphs.
- **Paragraphs:** 1–3 lines on landing pages.
- **Bullets:** Use for benefits, proof, steps, comparisons.
- **Headings:** Specific, not decorative. Bad: `Better Leads`. Better: `Only speak to homeowners already showing buying signals`.
- **Buttons:** Describe action + reward. Bad: `Submit`. Better: `Run My Free Scan`.
- **Proof:** Place next to the claim it supports.

---

## Page Architecture for Max Attention

1. **Hero** — Pain headline, outcome subhead, CTA, proof card, visual scan/result.
2. **Pain section** — What they lose: time, fuel, evenings, missed serious jobs, price shoppers, ghost quotes.
3. **Mechanism** — The data engine: planning applications, EPC signals, council work, building control, HMO licensing, contracts, local demand.
4. **Output** — What they receive: homeowner/project signal, location, project type, urgency score, suggested approach, letter/WhatsApp/email angle.
5. **Proof** — Sample postcode scan, screenshots, source labels, before/after journey, founder note.
6. **Pricing** — ROI framing: "One decent job pays for months of JobFilter."
7. **FAQ** — Remove final objections: shared leads, data source, legality, guarantees, cancellation, supported trades.

---

## The JobFilter Attention Rules

1. Never sound like generic SaaS.
2. Use trade language, not tech language.
3. Show local proof early.
4. Use numbers wherever possible.
5. Make the buyer feel the cost of doing nothing.
6. Let headings carry the argument.
7. Keep body text short.
8. Use one clear CTA.
9. Put proof beside claims.
10. Make the free scan feel instantly useful.

## Example Rewrite

Generic: `JobFilter helps tradespeople find qualified leads through intelligent data analysis and customer filtering.`

Stronger: `Stop quoting ghosts. JobFilter scans council and property data to show you homeowners already preparing serious building work.`

Sharpest: `Stop driving to ghost quotes. See who is actually building before your competitors call them.`

---

## Suggested A/B Tests

- **Test 1 — Pain vs Outcome headline:** `Stop driving to ghost quotes.` vs `Find serious building work before your competitors.`
- **Test 2 — CTA wording:** `Run My Free Scan` vs `Find Jobs Near Me` vs `Show Me Serious Leads`
- **Test 3 — Proof card placement:** beside hero / below CTA / after postcode input
- **Test 4 — Pricing frame:** `£22/month founding price` vs `One won job pays for years` vs `Less than one wasted quote per month`

---

## Final Principle

The page should not ask for attention. It should reward attention immediately.

A busy customer should be able to glance, understand, trust, and act.
